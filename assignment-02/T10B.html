Top 10 Books I've Ever Read

2023

AKA Top 10 Books YOU should read

As someone who read a lot, I have leagues of the best most well-written, soulful, gutwrenching prose that is ever to be dreamt of. And then I've read some of worst trash, bottom of the barrel, abominable books that has no place on any but the Devil's bookshelf. Having such experience gives me ample right to recommend books that almost anyoen should read!

All books range in genre, length, and enjoyability. Content warnings, summaries, and witty commentary (by yours truly) will all be provided! Oh, and fair warning: this list is embarrassingly self-indulgent, as all endeavors should be.

Ceremony by Leslie Marmon-Silko

Tayo, a young Native American, has been a prisoner of the Japanese during World War II, and the horrors of captivity have almost eroded his will to survive. His return to the Laguna Pueblo reservation only increases his feeling of estrangement and alienation. While other returning soldiers find easy refuge in alcohol and senseless violence, Tayo searches for another kind of comfort and resolution. Tayo's quest leads him back to the Indian past and its traditions, to beliefs about witchcraft and evil, and to the ancient stories of his people. The search itself becomes a ritual, a curative ceremony that defeats the most virulent of afflictions—despair. more...

This book is at the Top of my list for the best books of all time Marmon-Silkp is a master of mixing poetry and prose, weaving despair and earnest hope into the histories she writes. The characters are fully realized, their past driving them to achieve what they believe they want, and their hearts bringing them to achieve what they truly need. After reading this I was out of commition for a week straight. The writing blunt at times but never unkind in what it was saying. Definitely worth the read. Worth a thousand reads, in my personal opinion.

Smile by Raina Telgemeier

Raina just wants to be a normal sixth grader. But one night after Girl Scouts she trips and falls, severely injuring her two front teeth, and what follows is a long and frustrating journey with on-again, off-again braces, surgery, embarrassing headgear, and even a retainer with fake teeth attached. And on top of all that, there’s still more to deal with: a major earthquake, boy confusion, and friends who turn out to be not so friendly. This coming-of-age true story is sure to resonate with anyone who has ever been in middle school, and especially those who have ever had a bit of their own dental drama. more...

I read this book at a time in my life when I was just beginning to grow into I wasn't quite sure I liked. This book really helped with that. Telgemeier perfectly captures how finding yourself, your friends, and morals can be hard as a kid. This was an incredible book to read in middle school, and while we're all a lot older than that now, maybe your inner child needs a bit of healing. Not only a great read, but a quick one.

Lovecraft Country by Matt Ruff

Chicago, 1954. When his father Montrose goes missing, twenty-two year old Army veteran Atticus Turner embarks on a road trip to New England to find him, accompanied by his Uncle George—publisher of The Safe Negro Travel Guide—and his childhood friend Letitia. On their journey to the manor of Mr. Braithwhite—heir to the estate that owned Atticus’s great grandmother—they encounter both mundane terrors of white America and malevolent spirits that seem straight out of the weird tales George devours. At the manor, Atticus discovers his father in chains, held prisoner by a secret cabal named the Order of the Ancient Dawn—led by Samuel Braithwhite and his son Caleb—which has gathered to orchestrate a ritual that shockingly centers on Atticus. And his one hope of salvation may be the seed of his—and the whole Turner clan’s—destruction. more...

If you like H.P. Lovecraft, there's a great chance that you're going to enjoy this book. The is nothing like the cheap jumpscares that movies resort to these days. This is deep, instictual horror that builds and builds through each chapter until you're bursting with anticipation and dread. Here's the problem though: This book has an entirely black cast that exists in Jim Crow era America, which factors heavily into the horror of the book. To fully and correctly capture that experience, it's best to have first experienced it yourself (or have some cultural tie to it) but the author of the book does not. It's weird, because the book is phenomenal, but there's a handful of parts that rub me the wrong way due to the treatment or actions of some of the characters. The way that these specific characters are presented or treated come off as a shallow understanding of what that experience is or is just completely off the mark of whatever message is trying to be said. Overall the book is great but it's still good to mention it.

His Dark Materials Series by Phillip Pullman

Lyra is rushing to the cold, far North, where witch clans and armored bears rule. North, where the Gobblers take the children they steal--including her friend Roger. North, where her fearsome uncle Asriel is trying to build a bridge to a parallel world. Can one small girl make a difference in such great and terrible endeavors? This is Lyra: a savage, a schemer, a liar, and as fierce and true a champion as Roger or Asriel could want--but what Lyra doesn't know is that to help one of them will be to betray the other. more...

Okay, so I'm kind of cheating with this one since it's a series rather than a single book. But I don't think y'all would understand just how incredible the book is without reading the entire thing. Pullman masterfully meshes realistic fantasy with biblical theology to craft this triology, all the while arguing for intrinsic human goodness, freedom from state-mandated guilt, and and a love for the life we have now over an afterlife we hope to gain. My personal belief (that has yet to fail mme) is that an book banned by catholi church for heresy and blasphemy is a book worth reading, and these books? Definitely worth the read.

The Passage by Justin Cronin

First, the unthinkable: a security breach at a secret U.S. government facility unleashes the monstrous product of a chilling military experiment. Then, the unspeakable: a night of chaos and carnage gives way to sunrise on a nation, and ultimately a world, forever altered. All that remains for the stunned survivors is the long fight ahead and a future ruled by fear—of darkness, of death, of a fate far worse. more...

This book is not what it seems. Read that summary? it really just seems like some cheesy scifi novel you'd find in the airport convience store. But this book holds so. much. more. This book spans a hundred years, detailing the fall of civilization and it's rise after it, all while following a little girl who was there from the beginning. It's damn near poetry with how Cronin has written it. The one critiism I can give is that while it follows the one constant character of the book, the audience rarely gets to see her perspective or her own agency develope past that of the people around. Which I can see being hard to write since she starts off as like, six or eight years old in book, but it kind of seems like an issue that Cronin created for himself and should be fixed by him. While the book fails in that aspect, it hits the mark in every other category. Run don't walk to the bookstore to get this book.

Into The Wild by Jon Krakauer

In April, 1992, a young man from a good family hitchhiked to Alaska and walked alone into the wilderness north of Mt. McKinley. His name was Christopher Johnson McCandless. He had given $25,000 in savings to charity, abandoned his car and most of his possessions, burned all the cash in his wallet, and invented a new life for himself. Months later, a party of moose hunters found his decomposed body. How McCandless came to die is unforgettable. McCandless had roamed through the West and Southwest on a quest like those made by his heroes Jack London and John Muir. In the Mojave Desert he abandoned his car, stripped it of its license plates, and burned all of his cash. He would give himself a new name, Alexander Supertramp, and, unencumbered by money and belongings, he would be free to wallow in the raw, unfiltered experiences that nature presented. Leaving behind his desperate family, he vanished into the wild. more...

I'll be completely honest, when I first read this book, I hated it. It was a required reading for class and I hated anything approaching realistic fiction or biographical works. But after reading it a time or two, I grew to respect it. Christopher McCandless is an interesting and complex subject, and the journalistic approach to detailing his life and demise provides additional intrigue to his life. People argue about his reasons, what he was thinking, if he was a saint or just another arrogant asshole that thinks off-the-grid living is something anyone can do. But there's no real answers to the whys, and moralizing his life in anyway doesn't get us anywhere. The one truth is that he is a person, not some character that teaches us all a great lesson. And the book sticks to that theme till the end. Definitely an interesting read.

The Angel Experiment by James Patterson

Six unforgettable kids — with no families, no homes — are running for their lives. Max Ride and her best friends have the ability to fly. And that's just the beginning of their amazing powers. But they don't know where they come from, who's hunting them, why they are different from all other humans... and if they're meant to save mankind — or destroy it. more...

Oh, boy, is this a bad book. There had to be one on this list and it's gotta be this one. This book is the edgiest, not-like-the-other-girls, YA book to ever exist. Actually, I this book predates when YA started to really get popular. The plot makes no sense, every time you think it's over, it just keeps going, every action has to overdramatic in the best way, and it was cringe even for 2005. So why am I telling you to read this trash??? It's because the most fun you'll have on a Saturday afternoon. There are at least 12 books in this series and every single one an insane ride from start to finish. You literally cannot tell where any plot is going and it is amazing. It has all the vibes of Twilight or Divergent but with worse plots, more zany characters, more love triangles, and 10x the fun. I promise you, you will not regret reading this series, it's so bad it's good.

A Monster Calls by Patrick Ness

Conor has the same dream every night, ever since his mother first fell ill, ever since she started the treatments that don't quite seem to be working. But tonight is different. Tonight, when he wakes, there's a visitor at his window. It's ancient, elemental, a force of nature. And it wants the most dangerous thing of all from Conor. It wants the truth. Patrick Ness takes the final idea of the late, award-winning writer Siobhan Dowd and weaves an extraordinary and heartbreaking tale of mischief, healing and above all, the courage it takes to survive. more...

Ouch, this book hurts a lot. It deals with grief in such an interesting way, perfectly capturing how a twelve year old boy would handle his pain through the guise of fantasy. The blurring of imagination and reality is a useful metaphor of the relationships the main character has with the people around him. It's heartbreaking, but so worth the read. Books don't need happy endings, just hopeful ones.

Six of Crows by Leigh Bardugo

Ketterdam: a bustling hub of international trade where anything can be had for the right price—and no one knows that better than criminal prodigy Kaz Brekker. Kaz is offered a chance at a deadly heist that could make him rich beyond his wildest dreams. But he can’t pull it off alone. . . . A convict with a thirst for revenge, A sharpshooter who can’t walk away from a wager, A runaway with a privileged past, A spy known as the Wraith, A Heartrender using her magic to survive the slums A thief with a gift for unlikely escapes Six dangerous outcasts. One impossible heist. Kaz’s crew is the only thing that might stand between the world and destruction—if they don’t kill each other first. more...

This is such a heist book. It's like Ocean's 11 got eaten by Knives out and thrown threw the window J.R.R. Tolkein to send the message that this is how fantasy is done. The characters are endearing and despite how morally gray they are, you still root for them to achieve all their goals, even when they conflict with your OTHER favorite character's goals. Not a single person here is entirely good, and it hoenstly makes the story so much better for it. The world building is incredible and you get the feel that there's so much more history behind everything that's being said. This book is a spinoff of the Shadow and Bone series, but you really don't have to read it in order to understand this book. I didn't.

Ship It by Britta Lundin

CLAIRE is a sixteen-year-old fangirl obsessed with the show Demon Heart. FOREST is an actor on Demon Heart who dreams of bigger roles. When the two meet at a local fan panel, it's a dream come true for Claire. Until the Q&A, that is, when Forest laughs off Claire's assertion that his character is gay. Claire is devastated. She can't believe her hero turned out to be a closed-minded jerk. Forest is mostly confused that anyone would think his character is gay. Unfortunately, when the video of the disastrous Q&A goes viral, the producers have a PR nightmare on their hands. In order to help bolster their image within the LGBTQ+ community-as well as with their fans-they hire Claire to join the cast for the rest of their publicity tour. What ensues is a series of colorful Comic-Con clashes between the fans and the show that lead Forest to question his assumptions about sexuality and help Claire come out of her shell. ...more

Okay, now hear me out- WAIT, HEAR ME OUT- I know, okay? Fangirls? Shipping? obvious Supernatural-based show with an insane fan community? GAYS??? I get it. It's cringe. but remember, 1. this is my list, 2. cringe is dead, get over it, 3. I warned y'all that I'd be self-indulgent. This book exams the fandom behaviors in the early to mid-2010s in a funny and scarily realistic way, following the main character Claire, who is terminally online and a big part of the Demon Heart community on Tumblr. When I was younger, I was in similar fan spaces, and this book really spoke to me about some of the toxic behaviors that went on in the groupthink that was fandom during that time. But the book goes farther than that and speaks to how lighthearted and fun fandom can be. Friends, community, even love somehow were found in these fan spaces for people who often felt ousted from the people around them. And while it has it's drawbacks, it's not a bad thing. This book has an honest and positive view of fandom and how it affects both the fans and the actors.
